    URI http://resource.geosciml.org/classifier/cgi/lineationtype/0003

    ⇑ -- Crenulation lineation --

    URI http://resource.geosciml.org/classifier/cgi/lineationtype/0005
    Preferred label (en) Crenulation lineation
    Definition (en) Lineation defined by small-scale wrinkles, typically of phylosillicate minerals, on another surface, typically a cleavage or schistosity. Use instead of intersection lineation if the wrinkles are the measured feature and the intersecting planar feature is not apparent.

    URI http://resource.geosciml.org/classifier/cgi/lineationtype/0006

    URI http://resource.geosciml.org/classifier/cgi/lineationtype/0015
    Preferred label (en) Particle shape lineation
    Definition (en) Lineation defined by alignment of long axes of prolate-shaped particles such as clasts, fossils, crystals, or phenocrysts. Particles may be polycrystalline aggregates formed by deformation of pre-existing particles. General category does not specify genesis of particles or their alignment.

    ⇑ -- Pencil lineation --

    URI http://resource.geosciml.org/classifier/cgi/lineationtype/0016
    Preferred label (en) Pencil lineation
    Definition (en) Lineation defined by tendency of rock to break into long, slender pieces resembling pencils. Typically produced by intesection of bedding and cleavage or intersection of two cleavages. Fabric is generally only observed in weakly metamorphosed rocks.
    Source () based on Neuendorf et al 2005

    ⇑ -- Tectonic lineation --

    URI http://resource.geosciml.org/classifier/cgi/lineationtype/0022
    Preferred label (en) Tectonic lineation
    Definition (en) Penetrative linear structure in a rock body defined by fabric elements that are the product of tectonic processes. Linear is used in the sense that the orientation of the structure can be represented as a line in some sufficiently small neighborhood of any point in the material containig the fabric. Penetrative denotes that the fabric elements defining the structure are repeated at distances so small, compared with the scale of the whole…that they can be considered to pervade it uniformly and be present at every point (paraphrase of Turner and Weiss, 1963, p. 21).
    Source () this vocabulary
